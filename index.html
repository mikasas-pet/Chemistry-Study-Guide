Section 1 · Topics 2, 3, 9
Atomic Structure
Atomic RadiusNot on formula sheet — must memorize

The distance from the nucleus to the outermost electron. Think of it as the "size" of the atom.

Across a period → (left to right)
  • DECREASES
  • More protons = stronger nuclear pull
  • Electrons added to the same shell
  • Nucleus pulls them inward
Down a group ↓ (top to bottom)
  • INCREASES
  • Each new row adds a new electron shell
  • More inner shells = more shielding
  • Outer electrons pushed farther away
Key fact: Largest atoms are at the bottom-left of the periodic table (e.g. Francium, Caesium). Smallest are at the top-right (e.g. Helium, Fluorine).
ElectronegativityNot on formula sheet — must memorize

How strongly an atom pulls shared electrons toward itself during a bond. Think of it as "greediness" for electrons.

Across a period → (left to right)
  • INCREASES
  • More protons = stronger attraction for electrons
  • Same shell distance from nucleus
Down a group ↓ (top to bottom)
  • DECREASES
  • More electron shielding from inner shells
  • Shared electrons farther from nucleus
Fluorine (F) has the highest electronegativity of ALL elements on the periodic table. Electronegativity trends are essentially the reverse of atomic radius trends — bigger atom = weaker pull on shared electrons.
Finding Number of NeutronsNot on formula sheet — must memorize
Neutrons = Mass Number − Atomic Number (protons)
Example — Chlorine-35
Mass number = 35  |  Atomic number (from periodic table) = 17
Neutrons = 35 − 17 = 18 neutrons

Remember: Atomic number = protons = electrons (neutral atom). Mass number = protons + neutrons. Isotopes have the same number of protons but different neutrons.

Section 2 · Topics 1, 16
Bonding
Ionic vs Covalent BondsLikely in Communication section
Ionic Bond
  • Metal + Nonmetal
  • Electrons are TRANSFERRED
  • Creates + and − ions
  • High melting/boiling point
  • Conducts when dissolved or melted
  • Always solid crystal at room temp
  • Examples: NaCl, MgO, CaCl₂, KBr
Covalent Bond
  • Nonmetal + Nonmetal
  • Electrons are SHARED
  • Creates neutral molecules
  • Lower melting/boiling point
  • Does NOT conduct electricity
  • Can be solid, liquid, or gas
  • Examples: H₂O, CO₂, CH₄, HCl
Quick ID rule: Metal + nonmetal = IONIC. Two or more nonmetals = COVALENT. Look at the elements first — that's your instant shortcut on the exam.
Lewis Structures — 1 ionic & 1 covalentMust be able to draw both types
Ionic example: NaCl
  • Na has 1 valence electron → gives it to Cl
  • Draw Na⁺ with no dots, show the + charge
  • Draw [Cl with 8 dots]⁻ in square brackets with − charge
  • Both charges must be clearly labelled
Covalent example: H₂O
  • O is central atom (6 valence electrons)
  • Each H shares 1 electron with O
  • O ends up with 2 bonding pairs + 2 lone pairs = 8 electrons total
  • Each H has 2 electrons (H only needs 2)
Steps for any covalent Lewis structure
  1. Count total valence electrons for ALL atoms combined
  2. Place the least electronegative atom in the centre — H is NEVER central
  3. Connect all atoms with single bonds (each bond uses 2 electrons)
  4. Fill remaining electrons as lone pairs, starting with outer atoms first
  5. Check octet rule: each atom needs 8 electrons (except H which only needs 2)
Exam trap: Hydrogen (H) is NEVER the central atom. Common central atoms: C, N, O, S, P. If multiple options exist for central atom, pick the least electronegative one.
Section 3 · Topics 4, 6, 10, 18
Chemical Reactions
Types of Chemical ReactionsMust identify and write examples of all 5
Synthesis
A + B → AB
Two or more substances combine into one. Example: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
Decomposition
AB → A + B
One substance breaks apart into two or more. Example: 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂
Single Replace.
A + BC → AC + B
One element replaces another in a compound. Example: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu (Zn is more reactive than Cu)
Double Replace.
AB + CD → AD + CB
Two compounds swap partners. Example: AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl↓ + NaNO₃
Combustion
fuel + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O (+energy)
Burning in oxygen. Example: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
Neutralization (acid + base → salt + water) is a type of double replacement reaction. It always produces water and an ionic salt.
Complete vs Incomplete CombustionVery likely Communication section
Complete combustion
  • Plenty of O₂ available
  • Products: CO₂ + H₂O only
  • Maximum energy released
  • Clean blue flame
Incomplete combustion
  • Not enough O₂ available
  • Products: CO + C (soot) + H₂O
  • Less energy released
  • Yellow/orange, sooty flame
CO (carbon monoxide) produced by incomplete combustion is colourless, odourless, and extremely toxic. It binds to haemoglobin in blood, preventing oxygen transport — this is why car exhaust in an enclosed garage is deadly. This real-world context often appears in a Thinking question.
PrecipitateDefinition + how to identify

A precipitate is an insoluble solid that forms when two aqueous (aq) solutions react in a double replacement reaction. It drops out of solution and is labelled (s) or with a ↓ arrow.

Example
Pb(NO₃)₂(aq) + 2KI(aq) → PbI₂(s)↓ + 2KNO₃(aq)
PbI₂ is insoluble — it forms a solid precipitate that settles in the solution. The other product (KNO₃) remains dissolved.
How to predict: Use solubility rules. If a double replacement product is insoluble → precipitate forms and is marked (s). If both products are soluble → no reaction (write NR).
Net Ionic Equation3-step process — must know cold
  1. Write the fully balanced molecular equation with all compounds shown normally
  2. Write the complete ionic equation: split all soluble ionic compounds labelled (aq) into their individual ions. Keep (s), (l), (g), and weak acids/bases as whole, unsplit molecules.
  3. Identify and cancel spectator ions — ions that appear identically and unchanged on both sides. Everything that remains is the net ionic equation.
Full worked example
Molecular: AgNO₃(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO₃(aq)
Complete ionic: Ag⁺ + NO₃⁻ + Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → AgCl(s) + Na⁺ + NO₃⁻
Cancel spectators Na⁺ and NO₃⁻ (same on both sides)
Net ionic: Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s)
Never split: Solids (s), liquids (l), gases (g), weak acids (e.g. CH₃COOH), and weak bases. Only split strong electrolytes labelled (aq).
Section 4 · Topics 7, 8, 19, 21
Solutions & Concentration
Concentrated vs Dilute SolutionsConcept — short answer
Concentrated
  • High amount of solute relative to solution volume
  • High C value in mol/L
  • Example: strong espresso ☕
Dilute
  • Low amount of solute relative to solution volume
  • Low C value in mol/L
  • Example: watered-down juice 🧃
These are relative terms, not exact values. A "concentrated" solution of one substance might have a lower molarity than a "dilute" solution of another — it depends on context.
Dissociation vs IonizationKEY difference — very likely Communication section
Dissociation
  • IONIC compounds (e.g. NaCl, KBr)
  • Ions already exist in the solid
  • Water separates the existing ions
  • No new ions are created
  • NaCl(s) → Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)
Ionization
  • MOLECULAR/covalent compounds (e.g. HCl)
  • No ions exist before dissolving
  • Molecule reacts with water to create ions
  • Brand new ions are formed
  • HCl(g) + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻
The most testable difference: Dissociation SEPARATES ions that already existed in the crystal. Ionization CREATES new ions from a neutral molecule that had none. Know this cold — it's a guaranteed Communication or Thinking question.
Calculating Concentration (when volume is given)Formulas on exam sheet
Core formula
C = n/V     n = C × V     V = n/C
C = molarity (mol/L)  |  n = moles of solute  |  V = volume in LITRES
Dilution formula — use when adding water to lower concentration
C₁V₁ = C₂V₂
Units must match on each side. You can use both in mL as long as they're the same unit.
Most common mistake: Forgetting to convert mL → L. ALWAYS divide mL by 1000 before using C = n/V. This error alone can cost you the entire question.
Example
Find C for 0.5 mol of NaCl in 250 mL of solution:
V = 250 ÷ 1000 = 0.250 L  |  C = n/V = 0.5 / 0.250 = 2.0 mol/L
ppm and ppb (Parts Per Million / Billion)Formulas on exam sheet

Used for extremely trace concentrations — think pollutants in water, medications in blood, contaminants in food.

Formulas — on your sheet
ppm = (mass of solute / mass of solution) × 10⁶
ppb = (mass of solute / mass of solution) × 10⁹
Scale: ppm = parts per million (×10⁶)  |  ppb = parts per billion (×10⁹). Each step is 1,000× more dilute than the previous. ppb is used for things like lead in drinking water — amounts so small they can't be described in mol/L easily.
Section 5 · Topics 5, 11, 12, 13, 17
Stoichiometry & Chemical Formulas
Law of Definite ProportionsConcept — short answer
A pure compound always contains the same elements in the exact same ratio by mass, regardless of the sample size, its source, or how it was made. This ratio is fixed by its chemical formula.
Example
Water (H₂O) is always 11.19% hydrogen and 88.81% oxygen by mass — whether it comes from a tap, a river, a raindrop, or a laboratory. The H₂O formula locks the ratio permanently. A sample from Mars would have the same ratio.
Writing Ionic Chemical Formulas — Criss-Cross MethodFrom the GC only — must know the method
  1. Write the cation (positive ion) first, then the anion (negative ion) beside it
  2. Criss-cross the charges: the charge number (not sign) of the cation becomes the subscript on the anion, and vice versa
  3. Reduce subscripts to the lowest whole-number ratio
  4. If a polyatomic ion needs a subscript greater than 1, put it in parentheses: e.g. Ca(OH)₂
Example 1: Al³⁺ and O²⁻
Criss-cross → Al₂O₃  (already lowest ratio) → Al₂O₃
Example 2: Mg²⁺ and OH⁻
Criss-cross → Mg₁(OH)₂ → polyatomic in brackets → Mg(OH)₂
Classic trap: Ca²⁺ + O²⁻ → criss-cross gives Ca₂O₂ → MUST reduce → CaO. Equal charges always simplify to 1:1 ratio. Always simplify!
Calculating Percent YieldFormula on exam sheet
Formula — on your sheet
% yield = (actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100%
Actual yield
  • What you actually got in the lab
  • Always given to you in the problem
  • Usually less than theoretical
Theoretical yield
  • What you should get in perfect conditions
  • Calculated via stoichiometry from balanced equation and limiting reagent
Percent yield is always ≤ 100%. If you calculate more than 100%, you made an error. Real lab losses come from evaporation, spills, incomplete reactions, and measurement error.
Example
Theoretical yield = 80.0 g, Actual yield = 60.0 g
% yield = (60.0 / 80.0) × 100% = 75.0%
Empirical vs Molecular FormulaKnow the difference + how to find n
Empirical formula
  • Simplest whole-number ratio of atoms
  • Example: CH₂O
  • May not represent the actual molecule
  • Found from % composition data
Molecular formula
  • Actual number of atoms in one molecule
  • Example: C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose)
  • Always a whole-number multiple of empirical
  • Requires molar mass to determine
n = (molar mass of molecular formula) ÷ (molar mass of empirical formula)
Molecular formula = empirical formula × n. If n = 1, they are identical.
% Composition → Molecular Formula6-step process — high-value Thinking question
  1. Assume exactly 100 g sample — each percentage immediately becomes grams (e.g. 40% C = 40 g of C)
  2. Convert each element's mass to moles: n = mass ÷ molar mass
  3. Divide ALL mole values by the smallest mole value you calculated
  4. Round results to whole numbers → these are your empirical formula subscripts
  5. Calculate the molar mass of that empirical formula
  6. n = (given molecular molar mass) ÷ (empirical molar mass) → multiply all subscripts by n → molecular formula
Full worked example: 40% C, 6.7% H, 53.3% O — Molar mass = 180 g/mol
Step 1: 40 g C, 6.7 g H, 53.3 g O
Step 2: n(C) = 40/12 = 3.33  |  n(H) = 6.7/1 = 6.7  |  n(O) = 53.3/16 = 3.33
Step 3: Divide by 3.33 → C=1, H=2, O=1
Step 4: Empirical = CH₂O  |  Step 5: M(CH₂O) = 30 g/mol
Step 6: n = 180 / 30 = 6 → Molecular formula = C₆H₁₂O₆
Section 6 · Topics 15, 20
Gas Laws
Which Gas Law to Use?All formulas on exam sheet — know WHEN to pick each
Boyle's Law
P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
Temperature is CONSTANT. Pressure and volume change. They are inversely proportional — one increases as the other decreases.
Charles's Law
V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂
Pressure is CONSTANT. Volume and temperature change. They are directly proportional — both increase or both decrease together.
Gay-Lussac's Law
P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂
Volume is CONSTANT. Pressure and temperature change. They are directly proportional.
Combined Gas Law
P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂
Nothing is constant, OR use this when unsure. Works for every single gas law scenario as a universal backup.
The #1 gas law mistake: Forgetting to convert °C to Kelvin. K = °C + 273. Do this BEFORE any other step. Using Celsius will give a completely wrong answer every time.
Solving Any Gas Law Problem — Step by StepFormula given — problem setup must be known
  1. List all given values clearly: P₁, V₁, T₁, P₂, V₂, T₂ — and identify which variable you need to find
  2. Convert ALL temperatures to Kelvin first: K = °C + 273
  3. Make sure pressure units match on both sides (both kPa or both atm). Same for volume units (both L or both mL).
  4. Choose the appropriate law. When uncertain, use the Combined Gas Law — it covers all cases.
  5. Rearrange algebraically to isolate the unknown, then solve. Sanity check: does the answer make physical sense?
Boyle's Law example — T constant
2.0 atm, 4.0 L → compressed to 1.0 L
P₂ = P₁V₁/V₂ = (2.0×4.0)/1.0 = 8.0 atm
Charles's Law example — P constant
3.0 L at 27°C → heated to 127°C
T₁=300K, T₂=400K
V₂ = (3.0×400)/300 = 4.0 L
Section 7 · Topic 14
Acids & Bases
Arrhenius TheoryDefinitions — very likely in Communication section
Arrhenius Acid
  • Produces H⁺ ions in aqueous solution
  • Undergoes ionization with water
  • HCl(aq) → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)
  • H₂SO₄(aq) → 2H⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq)
  • HNO₃(aq) → H⁺(aq) + NO₃⁻(aq)
Arrhenius Base
  • Produces OH⁻ ions in aqueous solution
  • Undergoes dissociation in water
  • NaOH(aq) → Na⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)
  • KOH(aq) → K⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)
  • Ca(OH)₂(aq) → Ca²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq)
Arrhenius in one sentence: acid makes H⁺, base makes OH⁻. Know the definition and two examples of each. That's full marks on this topic.
Neutralization — acid + base always gives salt + water
HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)
The H⁺ from the acid combines with OH⁻ from the base → water. The remaining ions (Na⁺ + Cl⁻) form the salt NaCl.
